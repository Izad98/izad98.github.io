The Use of CRISPR Gene Editing in Treating Genetic Diseases

Mohamed Izad February 27, 2023 6 min read
Home / Blog / CRISPR Gene Editing

CRISPR gene editing is a crucial tool with the ability to transform the way we handle genetic illnesses. Genetic illnesses are caused by mutations in our DNA, which can contribute to a broad variety of symptoms and health issues. With CRISPR, researchers can now target and correct these mutations with an unprecedented level of precision.

What is CRISPR?

CRISPR — Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats — is a natural defence mechanism found in bacteria, repurposed by scientists as a molecular tool for editing DNA. Working alongside the Cas9 enzyme, CRISPR acts as a highly precise molecular "scissors," capable of locating, cutting, and modifying specific sequences within the genome.

Genetic Diseases in Focus

Many inherited conditions result from single-gene mutations — sickle cell anaemia, cystic fibrosis, Huntington's disease, and muscular dystrophy among them. Traditionally, these diseases could be managed but not cured. CRISPR introduces the possibility of correcting the root mutation itself, rather than merely treating its downstream effects.

Early clinical trials have demonstrated promising results. In sickle cell disease, CRISPR-based therapies have been used to reactivate foetal haemoglobin production — effectively compensating for the mutated adult haemoglobin gene. Patients in these trials have shown dramatic reductions in painful crises and hospital admissions.

The Mechanism of Action

The CRISPR-Cas9 system operates through two core components: a guide RNA (gRNA) designed to match the target DNA sequence, and the Cas9 enzyme that performs the cut. Once the gRNA locates the complementary DNA sequence, Cas9 creates a double-strand break. The cell's own repair machinery then either disables the gene or — when a DNA template is provided — replaces it with a corrected sequence.

Challenges and Ethical Considerations

Despite its promise, CRISPR-based therapy faces significant challenges. Off-target edits — where the system cuts unintended sequences — remain a concern, particularly for germline applications where changes would be heritable. Delivery mechanisms (getting the editing machinery into the right cells efficiently) and immune responses to Cas9 proteins are active areas of research. The ethical landscape, especially regarding embryo editing and equitable access, demands ongoing societal deliberation.

The Road Ahead

The approval of Casgevy, the first CRISPR-based therapy, by regulators in the UK and US in late 2023 marks a watershed moment. As delivery technologies improve and off-target prediction tools become more robust, CRISPR's role in treating genetic diseases will only expand — moving from rare monogenic conditions toward more complex, multifactorial diseases.
