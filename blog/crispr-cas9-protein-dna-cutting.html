The Mystery of CRISPR-Cas9: How Cas9 Enzymes Cut DNA

Mohamed Izad October 21, 2023 5 min read
Home / Blog / CRISPR-Cas9 Mechanism

Have you ever wondered how scientists can precisely edit DNA, like the cut-and-paste function in a word processor, but on a microscopic level? The answer lies within a revolutionary gene-editing tool called CRISPR-Cas9. In this article, you will delve into the enigmatic world of CRISPR-Cas9 protein — exploring how it locates, binds, and cuts specific sequences within the genome with remarkable precision.

The Origin of CRISPR

CRISPR — Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats — was first identified as part of the bacterial adaptive immune system. When bacteria survive a viral attack, they store fragments of the invader's DNA between these repeats. On re-exposure, the bacteria transcribe these sequences into RNA that guides the Cas9 protein directly to the viral DNA, destroying it. Scientists Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier, awarded the 2020 Nobel Prize in Chemistry, recognised this machinery could be repurposed as a programmable genome editor.

The Two-Component System

CRISPR-Cas9 works through two key molecular players. First, the single guide RNA (sgRNA) — a synthetic fusion of two RNA molecules that provides both a scaffold for Cas9 binding and a ~20-nucleotide targeting sequence complementary to the genomic site of interest. Second, the Cas9 protein itself — a large multidomain endonuclease that carries two nuclease domains (HNH and RuvC), each responsible for cutting one strand of the double-stranded DNA.

The Cutting Mechanism

Before cutting, Cas9 must verify the target. The protein first scans the genome for a short sequence called the Protospacer Adjacent Motif (PAM — typically 5'-NGG-3' for the most widely used Streptococcus pyogenes Cas9). On finding a PAM, Cas9 unwinds the local DNA and checks whether the adjacent sequence matches the guide RNA. If it does, a conformational change activates both nuclease domains, producing a blunt-ended double-strand break precisely 3 base pairs upstream of the PAM.

This break is not the end of the story. The cell responds with one of two repair pathways: Non-Homologous End Joining (NHEJ), which is error-prone and often introduces small insertions or deletions that disrupt gene function, or Homology-Directed Repair (HDR), which uses a provided DNA template to install a specific sequence change. The choice between these pathways determines whether the edit results in gene knockout or precise correction.

Applications Across Biology

Beyond gene therapy, CRISPR-Cas9 has transformed basic research — enabling genome-wide functional screens, the creation of disease models, and the development of diagnostic tools (such as SHERLOCK and DETECTR). In agriculture, CRISPR is being used to develop disease-resistant and climate-resilient crops. In oncology, CAR-T cell therapies enhanced by CRISPR editing are showing early clinical promise.

Precision, Limitations, and Ethics

The system is powerful but imperfect. Off-target cuts — where Cas9 edits unintended sites with partial sequence homology — remain a safety concern in clinical applications. Base editing and prime editing technologies have since emerged to reduce double-strand breaks and extend the range of editable mutations. Ethically, the germline editing of human embryos (as controversially performed in 2018) highlights the need for rigorous governance frameworks to accompany this technology's rapid advancement.
